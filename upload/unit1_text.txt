Unit Name
Unit 1: Planning Personal & Academic Futures
Unit Narrative 
Learners reflect on their personal, academic, and professional aspirations, making predictions and planning concrete steps for their near and long-term future.
Goal 
Students can make predictions and plans about personal, academic, and professional futures using future forms, reflecting on their goals and possible opportunities.
Unit Objectives
Main Communicative Objectives: 
	•	Can make predictions and plans about personal, academic, and professional futures using future forms, reflecting on their aspirations and possible opportunities abroad.
	•	Can propose and evaluate solutions to real or hypothetical problems (e.g., adapting to a new culture, managing finances, solving conflicts in a group project) using zero, first, and second conditionals.
	•	Can discuss hypothetical outcomes of past or present decisions using third conditionals, connecting language learning with life lessons and critical reflection.
	•	Can negotiate group decisions by using gerunds and infinitives accurately when proposing ideas, showing respect for others’ perspectives and prioritizing collaboration.
	•	Can reflect on their own adaptability, resilience, and creativity while projecting themselves into new and diverse cultural or professional scenarios.
	•	Can demonstrate intercultural empathy by evaluating how people from different backgrounds might imagine and plan for their futures. 
Human Skills: 
	•	Can demonstrate creativity and foresight by designing projects or scenarios that combine realistic planning with imaginative solutions.
	•	Can practice critical thinking by evaluating multiple alternatives to a problem and justifying decisions with logical reasoning.
	•	Can collaborate effectively in group projects, dividing roles and responsibilities while respecting diverse ideas and approaches.
	•	Can give and receive constructive feedback on proposed plans or imagined futures, focusing on clarity, feasibility, and intercultural sensitivity.
	•	Can apply decision-making skills by considering risks, benefits, and long-term consequences of actions in hypothetical contexts. 
Language in use 
(Target language)
Grammar Points Context of use (Authentic Situations)
Talking about personal preferences and everyday choices:
• Expressing likes, dislikes, and preferences in daily life.
• Making small personal decisions (e.g., choosing food, clothes, or weekend activities).
• Comparing two or more options when deciding what to do.
(e.g., “I prefer to stay home tonight.” / “I’d rather go out for dinner.”)

Discussing future plans and intentions:
• Sharing academic or personal goals with classmates or teachers.
• Describing actions students plan or intend to do in the near future.
• Setting objectives or commitments (e.g., study habits, fitness, or career plans).
(e.g., “I plan to start a new course next semester.” / “I intend to improve my English.”)

Considering and reflecting before making decisions:
• Talking about options they are thinking about but haven’t decided yet.
• Discussing possible changes or projects.
• Exploring hypothetical or “in-progress” decisions.
(e.g., “I’m considering studying abroad.” / “We’re considering moving next year.”)

Expressing excitement and emotional connection to future events:
• Showing enthusiasm or anticipation about upcoming experiences.
• Discussing things they are waiting or preparing for.
• Building positive emotional engagement when talking about personal plans.
(e.g., “I look forward to meeting my new classmates.” / “We look forward to our trip.”)

Comparing alternatives and negotiating in group discussions:
• Making group or team decisions in class projects or meetings.
• Expressing preferences politely and respectfully in discussions.
• Reaching consensus using language of preference and planning.
(e.g., “I’d rather focus on the presentation today.” / “We prefer to start early to have more time.”)

Lexical Content
Core Vocabulary (Suggested): 
	•	Decision-Making Verbs & Expressions: prefer to, would rather, consider doing, plan to, intend to, look forward to
	•	Expressions for Preferences and Opinions: I prefer to…, I’d rather…, I think…, I believe…, In my opinion…, I’m not sure…, I agree / disagree
	•	Expressions for Future Planning: I plan to…, I intend to…, I’m considering…, I look forward to…, I’m thinking of…, I’m going to…
	•	Nouns Related to Decisions and Plans: decision, plan, goal, intention, option, choice, alternative, idea
	•	Adjectives for Decision Contexts: difficult, important, easy, possible, realistic, personal, professional
	•	Connectors for Explaining Decisions:  because, so, therefore, that’s why, however, on the other hand
Extended Vocabulary (Optional/Challenge):
	•	Advanced Decision-Making and Planning Terms: objective, strategy, priority, commitment, outcome, consequence, motivation
	•	Formal / Academic Verbs and Collocations: make a decision, set a goal, weigh the options, take into account, reach an agreement, plan ahead, have second thoughts
	•	Adjectives Describing Personal Attitude Toward Decisions: decisive, indecisive, confident, hesitant, thoughtful, ambitious
	•	Expressions for Future Vision and Reflection: look forward to achieving…, be interested in doing…, plan on improving…, intend to focus on…, consider changing…

Proposed Assessment
Formative Assessment (Ongoing):
Assessment will be continuous and integrated across all stages of Kolb’s Experiential Learning Cycle.
Concrete Experience: Teacher observation checklists during pair and group interactions (e.g., The Great Weekend Dilemma, Life Decisions Café). Focus on students’ spontaneous use of decision-making expressions in authentic choices. Example evidence: “I’d rather stay home,” “I plan to study this weekend.”
Reflective Observation: Oral or written exit reflections where students analyze language choices and meanings (e.g., distinguishing between prefer to and would rather, or identifying when consider doing fits better than plan to). Example task: “Which expression did you use most today? Why?”
Abstract Conceptualization: Grammar maps, sentence-completion frames, or mini-quizzes where students explain rules and patterns (e.g., “We use to + verb with plan and intend,” “We use -ing after consider and look forward to”). Teacher checks conceptual understanding and accuracy.
Active Experimentation: Peer/self-assessment rubrics during performance tasks (e.g., Decision Board Meeting, Future Me: My Big Decision Pitch). Peers evaluate clarity, accuracy, and variety of expressions. Reflection questions include: “Did your partner use at least three decision-making expressions correctly?” “Was the message clear and natural?”

When setting up the course evaluation in Epik, please remember that only one summative assessment can be created per unit. This ensures alignment between the unit plan and the platform’s evaluation structure.

Summative assessment (Mid-unit): 
Task 1 – Personal Decision Journal: Students write a short reflective entry (6–8 sentences) describing a real decision or plan using at least four target expressions. Example: “I’m considering taking a new course. I’d rather study in the morning. I plan to finish my project soon, and I look forward to learning new things.”
Task 2 – Pair Conversation “Decision Talks”: Students work in pairs to discuss three short dilemmas provided by the teacher (e.g., study vs. work, travel vs. save money). They must negotiate and express their preferences using prefer to, would rather, plan to, and consider doing.
Criteria:
• Language Accuracy: Correct structure and appropriate use of target expressions.
• Fluency & Interaction: Natural flow, turn-taking, and mutual comprehension.
• Communicative Purpose: Clear expression of intention, preference, and reasoning.

Summative assessment (Final): 
Format: Integrated Performance Assessment – “My Big Decision Pitch”
Task: Each student prepares and delivers a 1–2 minute presentation describing an important personal, academic, or professional decision. The pitch must include at least five target expressions (one from each category: preference, intention, consideration, and anticipation). Example: “I’m considering moving abroad. I’d rather live in a small city. I plan to apply for a scholarship and intend to study environmental engineering. I look forward to meeting new people.”
After the pitch, two classmates ask follow-up questions using similar expressions (Would you rather study in the U.S. or Europe? / Do you plan to go alone?).
Follow-up Written Task: Each student writes a short paragraph summarizing their classmates’ ideas or explaining what they learned from others’ decisions. Example: “Many students plan to study abroad. Some would rather work before continuing university. Everyone looks forward to new experiences.”
Criteria:
• Language Accuracy: Correct grammatical use of prefer to, would rather, consider doing, plan to, intend to, and look forward to.
• Fluency & Coherence: Clear pronunciation, logical sequence of ideas, and natural tone.
• Content & Reflection: Ability to connect decisions to real-life goals and justify choices.
• Interaction: Active engagement during peers’ presentations through questions and comments.
• Connection to Real World: Application of learned structures in authentic, personally meaningful contexts (e.g., career planning, lifestyle choices, academic decisions).

Activity 1: A Weekend from the Future
The teacher begins by creating a vivid personal story, naturally using a variety of future expressions. "Let me tell you about my weekend. Tomorrow, I’m visiting my cousin. On Saturday, I’m going to clean my house. On Sunday, I’ll probably sleep all day!" 
Then ask: “What about you? What are your plans for the weekend? What are your definite plans and what are your less certain ideas?”
For the pair work, give each pair a few picture cards or ask them to draw quick icons of activities they plan to do (watch TV, cook, study, travel, etc.). Each student explains their weekend plans to their partner, making sure to share both definite arrangements and less certain ideas. The goal is simply to communicate the meaning of their future plans. Invite 2–3 pairs to share some interesting plans with the class.
As pairs speak, the teacher walks around and listens, noting which future forms the students are using naturally (e.g., going to, 'll, present continuous) for use in the next stage, but provides no explicit grammar correction or feedback at this time.
Teacher Notes: This activity fits the Concrete Experience stage because students directly experience the use of future forms by talking about their real, personal weekend plans before any formal explanation. The conversation makes the language meaningful, linking language to authentic personal experiences without drawing attention to the grammatical structures themselves.

Activity 3: Fortune-Teller Fair
Important note for teachers:  This activity is designed to create a lighthearted and playful atmosphere. Feel free to adapt the tone and delivery according to your own teaching style and comfort level. The goal is to engage students in spontaneous communication about the future, not to imitate a specific character or performance. Teachers can keep the “Fortune-Teller” theme fun and interactive while staying authentic to their classroom approach.

Set the Scene (Whole class): “Welcome to our Fortune-Teller Fair! I can see your future…” (wave your hands dramatically).
Write on the board:
“You will …”
“You’re going to …”
“You’re meeting … tomorrow.”
Explain: “Let’s tell each other’s futures!”
Pick one student and say playfully: “I see you will travel soon! You’re going to meet a famous person. And you’re visiting the beach next weekend.”
Ask: “Do you think it’s true?” (Students laugh and react).

Assessment / Feedback
Monitor pairs and note examples of correct and incorrect forms.
Give light, oral feedback after each rotation. 
Teacher Notes
This is Concrete Experience because students live a playful, social experience—predicting classmates’ futures—before analyzing grammar rules. The imaginative context makes “will,” “going to,” and “present continuous” tangible through humor and curiosity, promoting empathy, interaction, and spontaneous speaking.

Activity 1: Spot the Difference: How Sure Are You?
The teacher begins by saying: “In a previous activity we talked about our weekend plans. Some of you said, I’m going to visit my family, others said, I’ll visit my family. Let’s think: Do those sentences mean the same thing?”
Let students share ideas freely. Write both sentences on the board and ask:
“Which sounds more planned? Which sounds more spontaneous?”
Guide them to notice that going to expresses intention, while will expresses decision or prediction.
Observation Activity:
Distribute a short handout “Spot the difference”.
Ask students: “Read these sentences and highlight the ones that talk about plans already arranged in blue and predictions or decisions in yellow.”
In pairs, students compare highlights and justify their choices:
“Why did you choose that color?”
“What’s the difference between I’ll call you and I’m calling you?”
The teacher circulates, listening, and prompting reflection with guiding questions.
Class Reflection Chart:
Draw two columns on the board labeled Certain / Planned and Uncertain / Decided Now.

Certain
Planned and Uncertain
Decided Now




Have students come up and place examples from their sheet into the correct column.
As a class, reflect on how will, going to, and present continuous fit into these patterns.
Assessment / Feedback
Assess through observation: students’ ability to justify why each form fits a meaning. Praise reflection, not just accuracy: “Good thinking! You noticed that going to shows intention even if the plan isn’t fixed yet.”
Encourage metalinguistic awareness by asking: “Can anyone explain why we can’t say I’m going to help you now in that moment?”

Going to → plan or prediction with evidence.
Present continuous → fixed arrangement or timetable.
Teacher Notes:

Through categorization and discussion, learners construct mental frameworks that connect language form to meaning.

	•	“Which expressions use to + verb?”
	•	“Which use -ing after them?”
	•	“Which ones express stronger decisions or emotions?”
Discuss the patterns and meanings together. Elicit generalizations:
	•	to + verb → concrete decisions or logical plans.
	•	-ing → ongoing thoughts or emotional anticipation.
	•	would rather → emotional preference, often immediate.
Write final summarized “rules” on the board collaboratively.
Ask students to write one new original sentence for each category using their own lives.
Teacher Notes
In this activity students analyze form, meaning, and use through pattern discovery and guided induction. They articulate the grammatical “rules” of decision-making expressions, transforming prior experiences into conceptual knowledge.

Suggested assessment Instructions/ Kolb Cycle Assessment Integration
Concrete Experience (CE):

Assessment Tool: Teacher observation checklists and participation notes.
Instructions: During experiential tasks such as The Great Weekend Dilemma and Life Decisions Café, the teacher observes students’ spontaneous use of decision-making expressions while discussing personal preferences or dilemmas. Assessment focuses on whether students attempt to use target language naturally (e.g., I’d rather stay home, I prefer to visit my family, I plan to travel next week).
Teachers note frequency of participation, willingness to communicate, and engagement in realistic decision-making contexts rather than grammatical perfection.
Kolb Integration: At this stage, assessment measures active involvement and emotional engagement in authentic experiences. The goal is for students to live the language through meaningful choices, setting the foundation for reflection and analysis in later stages.
Reflective Observation (OB):

Assessment Tool: Guided reflection questions and noticing tasks.
Instructions: After discussions or listening tasks (e.g., Decision Detectives), students complete short written or oral reflections responding to prompts such as:
“Which expression shows stronger emotion: would rather or prefer to?”
“When do you use consider doing instead of plan to?”
“Which expressions did you use most during the activity? Why?”
Teacher collects reflections or conducts brief pair discussions, checking students’ ability to analyze their choices and recognize language patterns.
Kolb Integration: Assessment here focuses on whether learners can observe and articulate differences in form, meaning, and use of decision-making expressions. Reflection connects emotional experience (CE) to conceptual insight, developing awareness of nuance and tone.
Abstract Conceptualization (AC):


Assessment Tool: Grammar charts, sentence-building frames, and rule-completion exercises.
Instructions: Students complete short guided tasks where they categorize expressions by structure and meaning (e.g.,
| Expression | Structure | Meaning | Example |).
They also fill in missing parts of grammar frames or rewrite sentences to demonstrate understanding.
Example tasks:
Complete: “We use to + verb after _______ and _______.”
Choose the correct form: “I’m considering (to change / changing) my job.”
Teacher checks whether students can accurately express grammatical patterns and state the rule in their own words.
Kolb Integration: At this stage, assessment evaluates students’ ability to generalize from experience and reflection. Success is demonstrated when learners can explain how and why each expression is used transforming practice into conceptual understanding.
Active Experimentation (AE):

Assessment Tool: Performance-based rubrics for communicative projects and peer feedback.
Instructions: In final production tasks like Decision Board Meeting and Future Me: My Big Decision Pitch, students apply target language to new, personalized contexts.
They must use at least five decision-making expressions to express preferences, plans, and emotions (e.g., I’d rather work abroad. I intend to apply next semester. I look forward to meeting new people.).
Kolb Integration: Assessment at this stage measures transfer and flexible use whether students can apply decision-making language creatively and effectively in authentic, future-oriented contexts. Learners demonstrate they can transform understanding into real-world communication, completing the experiential cycle.
