Unit Name
Unit 2: Problem-Solving & Hypothetical Scenarios
Unit Narrative 
Learners explore real and hypothetical challenges in personal, academic, or intercultural contexts, proposing and evaluating solutions.
Goal 
Students can propose and evaluate solutions to real or hypothetical problems using zero, first, and second conditionals, reflecting on possible outcomes
Unit Objectives
Main Communicative Objectives: 
	•	Can make predictions and plans about personal, academic, and professional futures using future forms, reflecting on their aspirations and possible opportunities abroad.
	•	Can propose and evaluate solutions to real or hypothetical problems (e.g., adapting to a new culture, managing finances, solving conflicts in a group project) using zero, first, and second conditionals.
	•	Can discuss hypothetical outcomes of past or present decisions using third conditionals, connecting language learning with life lessons and critical reflection.
	•	Can negotiate group decisions by using gerunds and infinitives accurately when proposing ideas, showing respect for others’ perspectives and prioritizing collaboration.
	•	Can reflect on their own adaptability, resilience, and creativity while projecting themselves into new and diverse cultural or professional scenarios.
	•	Can demonstrate intercultural empathy by evaluating how people from different backgrounds might imagine and plan for their futures. 
Human Skills: 
	•	Can demonstrate creativity and foresight by designing projects or scenarios that combine realistic planning with imaginative solutions.
	•	Can practice critical thinking by evaluating multiple alternatives to a problem and justifying decisions with logical reasoning.
	•	Can collaborate effectively in group projects, dividing roles and responsibilities while respecting diverse ideas and approaches.
	•	Can give and receive constructive feedback on proposed plans or imagined futures, focusing on clarity, feasibility, and intercultural sensitivity.
	•	Can apply decision-making skills by considering risks, benefits, and long-term consequences of actions in hypothetical contexts. 
Language in use 
(Target language)
Grammar Points Context of use (Authentic Situations)
Talking about personal preferences and everyday choices:
• Expressing likes, dislikes, and preferences in daily life.
• Making small personal decisions (e.g., choosing food, clothes, or weekend activities).
• Comparing two or more options when deciding what to do.
(e.g., “I prefer to stay home tonight.” / “I’d rather go out for dinner.”)

Discussing future plans and intentions:
• Sharing academic or personal goals with classmates or teachers.
• Describing actions students plan or intend to do in the near future.
• Setting objectives or commitments (e.g., study habits, fitness, or career plans).
(e.g., “I plan to start a new course next semester.” / “I intend to improve my English.”)

Considering and reflecting before making decisions:
• Talking about options they are thinking about but haven’t decided yet.
• Discussing possible changes or projects.
• Exploring hypothetical or “in-progress” decisions.
(e.g., “I’m considering studying abroad.” / “We’re considering moving next year.”)

Expressing excitement and emotional connection to future events:
• Showing enthusiasm or anticipation about upcoming experiences.
• Discussing things they are waiting or preparing for.
• Building positive emotional engagement when talking about personal plans.
(e.g., “I look forward to meeting my new classmates.” / “We look forward to our trip.”)

Comparing alternatives and negotiating in group discussions:
• Making group or team decisions in class projects or meetings.
• Expressing preferences politely and respectfully in discussions.
• Reaching consensus using language of preference and planning.
(e.g., “I’d rather focus on the presentation today.” / “We prefer to start early to have more time.”)

Proposed Assessment
Formative Assessment (Ongoing):
Assessment will be continuous and integrated across all four stages of Kolb’s Experiential Learning Cycle.
The purpose is to observe how students progressively move from experiencing and reflecting to conceptualizing and using all four types of conditionals in authentic communication.
Concrete Experience: Tools: Teacher observation checklists and anecdotal notes during interactive tasks (e.g., What Happens If…?, Decision Day, If I Had a Million Dollars, The Time Machine).
Focus: Students’ spontaneous use of conditionals in meaningful situations.
Example evidence:
If I eat too much sugar, I get tired. (Zero)
If it rains tomorrow, I’ll stay home. (First)
If I were rich, I’d travel the world. (Second)
If I had studied, I would have passed. (Third)
Teacher looks for:
Attempts to use conditional patterns without prompting.
Clear cause–effect or hypothetical reasoning.
Engagement and emotional connection with the context (personal stories, decisions, regrets).
Reflective Observation: Tools: Oral reflections, short written responses, and guided discussion prompts (e.g., Reality or Imagination?, Regret Detective).
Focus: Students’ ability to identify meaning differences among conditional types.
Example prompts:
“Which conditional talks about facts that are always true?”
“Which one shows something you can’t change?”
“Which structure uses would? Why?”
Evidence of learning:
Students can explain when and why we use each conditional (real, possible, imagined, or unreal past).
Abstract Conceptualization: Tools: Grammar maps, completion frames, and mini-quizzes (e.g., The Conditional Machine, Grammar Architects).
Focus: Students’ conceptual understanding of structure and function.
Example task:
Complete: If + ____ → would + ____ (Second)
Explain: “We use this structure for imaginary situations in the present.”
Match conditional type with meaning (real, hypothetical, or unreal past).
Teacher checks for:
Recognition of patterns: If + present → will + base verb / If + past → would + base verb / If + had + past participle → would have + past participle.
Ability to articulate the function of each type in their own words.
Active Experimentation: Tools: Peer/self-assessment rubrics during communicative tasks (e.g., If I Ruled the World, If I Were You Show, Change the Ending, What If History Had Changed?).
Focus: Application and fluency in spontaneous production using a mix of conditional types.
Example self-reflection questions:
“Did I use at least one example of each conditional correctly?”
“Did my sentences show real, possible, or imaginary situations clearly?”
“Did I help my partner understand through examples or context?”
Summative assessment (Mid-unit): 
Task 1 – “Conditional Reflections Journal” Format: Individual written task.
Students write a short 8–10 sentence reflective entry connecting different conditionals to their lives. They must include at least one sentence of each type.
Criteria: Language Accuracy: Correct structure and appropriate use of all four conditionals.
Meaning & Context: Sentences reflect authentic, personally meaningful examples.
Clarity & Coherence: Logical sequencing and use of connectors (so, because, then, as a result).
Reflection: Demonstrates awareness of real vs. unreal situations and their emotional impact.
Task 2 – Pair Dialogue “What If…? Conversations”
Format: Pair oral assessment.
Students discuss teacher-provided prompts mixing all conditional types. Pairs prepare short dialogues (4–5 exchanges each) demonstrating understanding of function and tone. Criteria:
• Language Accuracy: Correct structure and appropriate use of target expressions.
• Fluency & Interaction: Natural flow, turn-taking, and mutual comprehension.
• Communicative Purpose: Clear expression of intention, preference, and reasoning.

Summative assessment (Final): 
Integrated Performance Assessment – “If Life Were Different”
Format: Individual presentation + follow-up discussion.
Task: Each student prepares and delivers a 3–4 minute oral presentation describing how real life, personal experiences, or world events could be different using all four conditionals.
Suggested outline:
	•	Real Fact (Zero) – If people eat healthy food, they feel better.
	•	Real Possibility (First) – If I pass my exams, I’ll celebrate with my friends.
	•	Imagined Situation (Second) – If I were the president, I’d make university free.
	•	Unreal Past (Third) – If I had studied abroad, I would have learned another language.
After each presentation, two classmates ask follow-up questions using conditionals (What would you do if…? / What would have happened if…?).
Follow-up Written Task: Students write a short paragraph summarizing what they learned from two classmates’ ideas. Example:
“If my partner had studied medicine, she would have become a doctor. I learned that many of us would travel if we had more time.”

Activity 4: “Grammar Detective: The Case of the Unreal If”
Set the scene tell students that during the coming activity they are detectives. Their mission: discover the grammar behind sentences that talk about imaginary situations.
Read, write or display the following sentences:
	•	“If I were a boy, I think I could understand.” 
	•	“If I had wings, I would fly.”
	•	“If I were you, I’d take the job.”
	•	“If we didn’t have homework, we’d be happy.”
	•	“If I had a time machine, I’d visit the future.”

Ask students to create three columns on their notebooks:
| Sentence | What is real? | What is imaginary? | What do you notice? |
They underline if and the verb after it, then discuss what’s real and what’s not.

	•	How is it different from the First Conditional?
	•	What did I notice today?
Teacher Notes:
This activity exemplifies Reflective Observation because students analyze real-life language samples and discover through observation that Second Conditionals describe imaginary or impossible situations. The detective role enhances engagement while maintaining focus on noticing patterns rather than rule memorization.

Activity 1 – “The Conditional Machine” Begin by reminding students about the previous activities where they have talked about what happens when something is always true and what happens when something might happen. Tell them that they will the rules behind it like engineers building a machine.
On the board, draw two boxes labeled Zero Conditional and First Conditional.
Give each group mixed sentence strips. Ask students to sort these sentences into two machines: facts (always true) and real possibilities (future).
Groups read, discuss, and place each strip under the correct label. Walk around and listen for reasoning 
Rule Construction (Whole Class)
Once sorted, guide discovery asking questions like:
