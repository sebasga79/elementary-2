Unit Name
Unit 3: Imagining & Negotiating Futures
Unit Narrative 
Learners reflect on past choices and imagined futures, discussing hypothetical outcomes and negotiating ideas collaboratively.
Goal 
Students can discuss hypothetical outcomes of past or present decisions using second and third conditionals, and negotiate group decisions respecting others’ perspectives.
Unit Objectives
Main Communicative Objectives: 
	•	Can make predictions and plans about personal, academic, and professional futures using future forms, reflecting on their aspirations and possible opportunities abroad.
	•	Can propose and evaluate solutions to real or hypothetical problems (e.g., adapting to a new culture, managing finances, solving conflicts in a group project) using zero, first, and second conditionals.
	•	Can discuss hypothetical outcomes of past or present decisions using third conditionals, connecting language learning with life lessons and critical reflection.
	•	Can negotiate group decisions by using gerunds and infinitives accurately when proposing ideas, showing respect for others’ perspectives and prioritizing collaboration.
	•	Can reflect on their own adaptability, resilience, and creativity while projecting themselves into new and diverse cultural or professional scenarios.
	•	Can demonstrate intercultural empathy by evaluating how people from different backgrounds might imagine and plan for their futures. 
Human Skills: 
	•	Can demonstrate creativity and foresight by designing projects or scenarios that combine realistic planning with imaginative solutions.
	•	Can practice critical thinking by evaluating multiple alternatives to a problem and justifying decisions with logical reasoning.
	•	Can collaborate effectively in group projects, dividing roles and responsibilities while respecting diverse ideas and approaches.
	•	Can give and receive constructive feedback on proposed plans or imagined futures, focusing on clarity, feasibility, and intercultural sensitivity.
	•	Can apply decision-making skills by considering risks, benefits, and long-term consequences of actions in hypothetical contexts. 
Language in use

Grammar Points Context of use (Authentic Situations)
1. Describing how past decisions affect the present
Context of Use:
• Reflecting on personal life choices and their impact on current situations.
• Discussing academic or professional decisions and the consequences felt today.
• Analyzing “turning points” in life when imagining different outcomes.
Examples:
“If I had chosen another major, I would be studying something different now.”
“If I had saved more money last year, I would feel less stressed today.”
2. Imagining how present realities could change past outcomes
Context of Use:
• Considering how current habits, abilities, or personality traits might have influenced past results.
• Reflecting on performance in previous projects, exams, or personal events.
• Exploring self-improvement by linking current behavior to past outcomes.
Examples:
“If I were more organized now, I would have finished my assignment last week.”
“If I were more confident today, I would have spoken during the meeting yesterday.”
3. Reflecting on personal growth and hypothetical self-development
Context of Use:
• Connecting present strengths or weaknesses with imagined past achievements.
• Discussing areas of improvement for academic, social, or personal life.
• Building self-awareness through hypothetical reflection.
Examples:
“If I were better at managing stress, I would have enjoyed the semester more.”
“If I had known how to study effectively, I would feel more prepared today.”
4. Discussing alternative versions of events in storytelling
Context of Use:
• Engaging in creative storytelling or “parallel universe” scenarios.
• Reimagining events (family, academic, social) with different outcomes.
• Enhancing narrative skills by exploring consequences of altered actions.
Examples:
“If I hadn’t missed the bus, I wouldn’t be rushing right now.”
“If I weren’t sick today, I would have gone on the field trip last Friday.”
5. Analyzing real-life conditional logic in group discussions
Context of Use:
• Participating in academic debates about decisions, consequences, or social issues.
• Evaluating hypothetical solutions to community, environmental, or social problems.
• Using respectful, thoughtful language to negotiate and justify ideas in groups.
Examples:
“If the city had invested earlier in public transport, people would have fewer problems now.”
“If young people were more active today, they would have avoided many health issues earlier.”
6. Negotiating present and future decisions using hypothetical thinking
Context of Use:
• Using mixed conditionals to consider alternatives before making decisions.
• Discussing what could change in the future if past or present conditions were different.
• Supporting decision-making with hypothetical analysis in class projects or teamwork.
Examples:
“If we had started the project sooner, we would be more relaxed now.”
“If we were more prepared today, we would have avoided the confusion last week.”

	•	Decision-Making Verbs & Expressions: prefer to, would rather, consider doing, plan to, intend to, look forward to
	•	Expressions for Preferences and Opinions: I prefer to…, I’d rather…, I think…, I believe…, In my opinion…, I’m not sure…, I agree / disagree
	•	Expressions for Future Planning: I plan to…, I intend to…, I’m considering…, I look forward to…, I’m thinking of…, I’m going to…

	•	Adjectives for Decision Contexts: difficult, important, easy, possible, realistic, personal, professional
	•	Connectors for Explaining Decisions:  because, so, therefore, that’s why, however, on the other hand

	•	Formal / Academic Verbs and Collocations: make a decision, set a goal, weigh the options, take into account, reach an agreement, plan ahead, have second thoughts
	•	Adjectives Describing Personal Attitude Toward Decisions: decisive, indecisive, confident, hesitant, thoughtful, ambitious
	•	Expressions for Future Vision and Reflection: look forward to achieving…, be interested in doing…, plan on improving…, intend to focus on…, consider changing…

Use of target structures naturally in context, even with partial accuracy.

Reflective Observation (RO): Awareness & Self-Explanation
Assessment Focus:
Ability to notice patterns in their own language use.
Contributions to reflection discussions, such as during Looking Back at Our Alternate Lives.
Ability to describe the time relationships in mixed conditionals (past → present / present → past).
Ability to articulate differences between gerund/infinitive proposal patterns.
Students identify challenges: “I find it hard to connect past and present ideas.”
Abstract Conceptualization (AC): Pattern Recognition & Rule Formation
Assessment Focus:
Students’ ability to explain mixed conditional logic during The Logic Map activity.
Ability to generalize rules for proposals (gerund vs infinitive) without memorization.
Accuracy when describing form and meaning relationships.
Teacher informally checks comprehension by asking guiding questions and listening to student explanations.
Active Experimentation (AE): Application in New Situations
Assessment Focus:
Functional use of grammar and vocabulary in new communicative contexts such as Decision Lab, Alternate Reality Debate, or Proposal Shark Tank.
Ability to negotiate, justify decisions, and express consequences clearly.
Fluency, clarity, and appropriateness of structure choice in extended speaking tasks.
Effective negotiation language: “I’d rather choose the internship because it opens opportunities…”
Teacher listens for spontaneous, accurate, and meaningful use of the structures.

PART 1 — Individual Spoken Task (5–6 minutes per student)
Task: “Your Alternate Future Plan & Decision Analysis”
Students answer three prompts, demonstrating grammar + vocabulary integration:
Prompt A — Future Plans & Goals (Gerunds/Infinitives)
“You are planning your next academic or professional step. Tell me your plan, your alternative options, and why you prefer one over the others.”
Prompt B — Mixed Conditional Reflection
“Think of one past decision that has affected your present. Explain the connection using a mixed conditional.”
Prompt C — Problem-Solving Proposal (Gerunds/Infinitives + Mixed Conditionals)
“Imagine your friend is facing an obstacle related to school, work, or cultural adjustment. Propose a solution and explain how things would change.”

PART 2 — Small Group Task (10–12 minutes)
Task: “Design a Realistic Plan + Alternate Reality Outcome”
In groups of 3–4, students must:
1. Create a future plan together (gerunds/infinitives)
We’d like to create a volunteering project.
We plan to propose a sustainability initiative.
We’re considering organizing a cultural exchange day.
2. Identify a challenge and propose solutions (unit vocabulary)
Using vocabulary such as:
challenge, obstacle, solution, opportunity, adaptability, diversity
3. Produce mixed conditional reflections
One for each pattern:
Past → Present:
“If we had started earlier, we would feel more prepared now.”
Present → Past:
“If we were more organized today, we would have completed the plan last week.”
4. Present their plan orally to the class
A clear, 2–3 minute mini-presentation.

PART 3 — Reflective Micro-Write (5 minutes, spoken or written)
Prompt:
“Summarize how your ideas, plans, or worldview would change if one past event in your life had been different.”
Expected language:
	•	If I had… I would…
	•	future-plan vocabulary
	•	problem-solving vocabulary
	•	decision-making expressions
This confirms internalization of mixed conditionals + integrated vocabulary.

Activity 1 – “The Island Decision Game” Set the scene tell students “Imagine this, your plane crashed on a deserted island. You can only keep three items from the wreck.” Write on the board: - If we had more food, we would survive longer. - If we had known about the storm, we wouldn’t have taken the flight. Ask: “What do these sentences express?” Guide them to notice they talk about imaginary or unreal situations. Give each group a set of “Island Survival Cards.” Then tell students to discuss and decide which three items their group will keep. they must all agree.
Encourage use of target language by writing on the board: If we took the map, we could find help faster. If we hadn’t lost the radio, we might have called for rescue.
Each group presents its three chosen items and justifies their decision.
After presentations, ask: “Which group made the most logical decisions?” “Did everyone agree in your group? How did you solve disagreements?”
Teacher Notes This Concrete Experience immerses students in a realistic and emotional scenario, a survival crisis requiring immediate collaborative decisions. Learners experience the meaning of hypothetical situations before formal analysis. The vivid context and social negotiation engage empathy, teamwork, and problem-solving, laying the foundation for later reflection and conceptualization.

Activity 3 – “What Did We Really Say?”
The teacher begins by saying: “In a previous activity we made plans for the weekend. Let’s think about what we said.” Read several real examples from students: I suggest go to the beach, I’d like to watch a movie, We decided going to the park.
Ask: “Do all of these sound natural to you? Which ones sound ‘English-like’? Which don’t?”
Write both correct and incorrect versions on the board in two columns.
Example:
✅ I suggest going to the beach.
❌ I suggest to go to the beach.
Ask students to identify patterns:
“What verb comes after suggest?” (verb + -ing)
“What verb comes after decide?” (to + verb)
Highlight that different verbs are followed by different patterns.
Pair reflection
- Which verbs did you use most to make proposals?
- Which ones were followed by -ing? Which by to + verb?
How did using these forms help you sound polite or clear?
Students discuss in pairs and write examples from their own speech.
Groups share observations. Teacher organizes findings on the board into two categories, created collaboratively:
Verbs + -ing	Verbs + to + verb
suggest, recommend, enjoy, avoid	decide, plan, want, would like
Ask: “Why do you think English uses different forms?”
“How do these verbs help us make polite proposals?”
Teacher Notes 
In this task students analyze and compare what they produced during the Concrete Experience. They move from spontaneous use to conscious noticing  identifying which structures worked and why. The focus is on reflection, not rule memorization, bridging experience with conceptual understanding.

Activity 4 – “Language Detective Journals”
The teacher reconnects to experience saying: “In our movie night plans, you made great proposals. Now you are detectives your mission is to discover how English expresses proposals through patterns.”
Provide examples from memory or from the board:
	•	I’d love to watch a comedy.
	•	How about watching an action film?
	•	We decided to order pizza.
Ask: “Do you see any patterns? What comes after each verb?”
In pairs, students highlight all verbs and the forms that follow them.
suggest → watching
decide → to watch
enjoy → going
want → to go
Teacher circulates, prompting awareness:
	•	“Which verbs take -ing? Which take to + verb?”
Ask students:
	•	What similarities do you see among verbs that use -ing?
	•	When do we use to + verb?
	•	How did using these expressions make communication easier or harder?
	•	What would you change next time to be clearer or more polite?
Then share answers in small groups and summarize key findings.
Collect discoveries and write two rules students propose themselves, e.g.:
	•	“After suggest or recommend, use -ing.”
	•	“After decide or want, use to + verb.”
Teacher Notes
This is a Reflective Observation activity because it invites learners to step back from communication, analyze patterns, and verbalize their insights. Instead of learning rules directly from the teacher, they infer them through guided discovery. The reflective journaling consolidates awareness and prepares students for the next stage, Abstract Conceptualization, where rules are systematized and formalized.

Type A – Unreal past → Hypothetical present
If + had + past participle → would + base verb
(e.g., If I had slept more, I would feel better now.)
Type B – Unreal present → Hypothetical past

(e.g., If I were more confident, I would have spoken in the meeting.)
These two types are both mixed conditionals, but they express different logic.”
Ask students to explain the meaning in their own words on the walltalkers:
